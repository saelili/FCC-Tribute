Caroline
Earle White

1833-1916

Caroline Earle White was a dedicated activist and advocate for animals in a time when the movement for humane treatment was just beginning to take form. She had always been disturbed by the treatment of work horses in Philadelphia, and after meeting with early animal advocates she co-founded the Pennsylvania Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals in 1867. Despite being a founder she was not permitted to hold a leadership role as a woman and so went on to found its women’s branch in 1869. Guided by an all-female board of directors, the Women’s Pennsylvania SPCA soon became a force in the humane community through campaigns and legislation combined with direct care for local animals.

White continued her work in animal advocacy throughout her life and kept her position as president of the WPSPCA until her death at the age of 83. She was also involved in literary societies, women’s suffrage, and worked with organizations that helped the poor obtain medical services. Her achievements encouraged women to pursue roles in society typically occupied by men.

"When it comes to that hour of your life, it will be a great consolation to feel that you always protected the poor, the helpless, and the unfortunate; and that you excercised a particular care towards animals."

WPSPCA horse drinking station in 1911

The WPSPCA erected drinking fountains around Philadelphia for the use of working people, horses, and small animals. Many were inscribed with mottos intended to inspire kinder treatment towards animals. Some still exist today and a few are still operational. A map of the remaining fountains can be found here.

A horse receiving medical treatment

Horses were a large part of early anti-cruelty actions. 70% of all cruelty cases from 1870-1890 were horse related. Caroline called on her members to boycott cruel carriage horse companies and even go as far as to citizen arrest malicious drivers. In 1909, the group, along with other city humanitarians, secured legislation forbidding the sale or purchase of disabled work horses.

International Anti-Vivisection Congress in 1913

In 1871, a physician wrote to White requesting that the WPSPCA turn over dogs from the shelter for use in research. White called a meeting which resulted in a resolution protesting vivisection. This event and later meetings with British anti-vivisectionists led her to found the American Anti-Vivisection Society in 1883. It was the first organization of its kind in the United States.

History of the SPCA

  • 1821
    Maine engages first anti-cruelty law; a dozen states have similarly followed by 1860. But cruelty of domestic animals resulted in only small fines or a few days in jail.
  • 1822
    England gets national anti-cruelty legislation and the SPCA – first in the world. In 1840 name was changed to the Royal SPCA.
  • 1866
    ASPCA starts in NYC and continues to serve animals today along with a strong national anti-cruelty mission. The PSPCA follows in PA in 1867, then MASPCA in MA in 1868.
  • 1869
    Women’s branch of PSPCA is started in Philadelphia, later separates from the PSPCA, called the Women’s PSPCA in 1897. Today is known as the Woman’s Humane Society.
  • 1874
    Morris Refuge for Homeless and Suffering Animals opens in Philadelphia in 1874, the first cat shelter in the U.S., by Elizabeth Morris with financial help by the Women’s branch of PSPCA. It continues to operate today at 1242 Lombard Street.
  • 1870
    Controversy erupts when a prominent physician requests all strays from the new shelter in Philadelphia. Congress passes the first national animal-protection law, protecting farm animals.
  • 1877
    American Humane Association is founded to have one unified voice in the humane movement when 27 humane organizations from 10 states meet in Cleveland, Ohio. A national federation of societies, the oldest organization dedicated to ending abuse and neglect for both children & animals.
  • 1883
    The American Anti-Vivisection Society began in Philadelphia, the first of its kind in the United States.
  • 1889
    Ryerss’ Infirmary opens in Philadelphia area as the nation’s first retirement and infirmary for workhorses. It continues to exist today as Ryerss Farm for Aged Equines.
  • 1907
    Women’s Philadelphia SPCA works to ensure compliance to new laws to treat cattle in transit.
  • 1954
    Humane Society of the U.S. starts in Washington, D.C. and is America’s largest animal protection organization, working with legislation, industry reform, and more, it is a mainstream force against cruelty and supplements local efforts today.

A group of children in the Band of Mercy

White's efforts also extended to education. She believed in the use of early intervention to combat the cruelty towards animals that was taken for granted in her time. Working with schools, she educated children on kindness towards animals through the Bands of Mercy program that was quickly gaining worldwide popularity.

The WPSPCA dispensary, pictured in 1927

In December of 1909 the Women's Humane Society opened the first dispensary for horses and small animals on Chadwick Street in Philadelphia. It was called the Caroline Earle White Dispensary in recognition of her contributions to the the animal welfare movement. Sick and injured animals were treated free of charge.

The current Veterinary Hospital logo

The Women’s Humane Society still operates today, 147 years after White founded it in 1869. They operate a shelter for dogs and other small animals, provide adoption and training services for pet owners in the region, and continue to fight against animal abuse through education. The Caroline Earle White Veterinary Hospital still provides care for domestic animals.

When asked what changes she was most proud of bringing about, Caroline Earle White described the 28-Hour law that the Women’s PSPCA spearheaded. The move toward this legislation came about when organization became aware of inhumane treatment of animals in transport. Under her leadership, the Women’s PSPCA stationed agents at specific locations along the major railroad routes to observe how the animals were handled. They kept records by telegraphing back to headquarters what they observed on the rail lines. Eventually they had a strong enough case to take the Reading Railroad to court in 1896 for transporting a shipment of horses over a 52-hour period without ever stopping to feed them or give them water. They won their case and in 1907 were able to pass legislation that mandated that animals be fed and watered after 28 hours in transit. Fines and convictions of various train lines followed and the legislation is still in place today.
